Readings

Course Readings

Readings are organized by day, with Required (current, short, do-before-class) and Recommended (deeper or historical) tiers.

  • Links go to the source where it is freely available online.
  • [Find it] links run a title search (use them if a direct link is paywalled or moves).
  • [Provided] items are distributed through the course materials — these include copyrighted articles and book excerpts we don’t post publicly.

No textbook purchase is required. Co-Intelligence is recommended, not required; its core ideas are captured in Mollick’s free essays and newsletter (below).

Pre-Course (Optional but Recommended)

  1. Mollick, E. (2025). I, Cyborg: Using Co-Intelligence. One Useful Thing. Read free
    • The free, current stand-in for the book: the jagged frontier, “centaurs vs. cyborgs,” and keeping a human in the loop.
  2. World Health Organization. (2024). Ethics and governance of AI for health: Guidance on large multi-modal models. WHO. Available online
    • The updated, generative-AI-era successor to WHO’s 2021 guidance; read the executive summary.
  3. Recommended: Topol, E. (2019). High-performance medicine. Nature Medicine, 25(1), 44–56. Available online · “How People Are Really Using AI in 2026.” [Provided]

Day 1 — Foundations of AI

Required

  1. Anthropic. (2024). Prompt Engineering Guide. Available online
    • Focus on “Be clear and direct” and “Use examples.”
  2. Anthropic. (2025). Effective context engineering for AI agents. Available online
    • Why the course now centers context engineering over single prompts.

Recommended

  1. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence, Ch. 1–2. Portfolio/Penguin. [Provided]
  2. Vaswani, A., et al. (2017). Attention is all you need. NeurIPS 30. Available online — historical context only.
  3. Governor Shapiro. (2024). Executive Order on AI governance for the Commonwealth. Available online
  4. MIT Technology Review. (2026). 10 Breakthrough Technologies / What matters in AI right now. Find it — a quick snapshot of how fast things changed.

Day 2 — Multimodal AI & Working with Models

Required

  1. Jowsey, T., et al. (2025). We reject the use of generative AI for reflexive qualitative research. Available online
    • The current debate on AI in qualitative analysis; pairs with Activity 4.
  2. Boykis, V. (2026). Running local models is good now. Read free (Find it) — open/local models for sensitive data.

Recommended

  1. OpenDataPhilly. (2024). Heat Vulnerability Index — Metadata & Documentation. Available online — review before Activity 3.
  2. Gichoya, J. W., et al. (2022). AI recognition of patient race in medical imaging. Lancet Digital Health, 4(6). Available online
  3. “Exploring Agent-Assisted Qualitative Analysis.” [Provided] — the constructive counterpoint to Jowsey et al.
  4. Anthropic. (2025). Coding agents for data analysis. [Provided] / Find it
  5. Meskó, B., & Topol, E. (2023). Regulatory oversight of LLMs in healthcare. npj Digital Medicine, 6. Available online — now largely superseded by WHO (2024).
  6. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence, Ch. 3. [Provided]

    • What building a real agent actually takes.

  3. Anthropic / modelcontextprotocol.io. Introduction to the Model Context Protocol (MCP). Available online

  1. Google. (2025). Google AI Studio Documentation. Available online — review before Activity 4.
  2. Ollama. (2025). Ollama Documentation. Available online — for Activity 6.

  4. Forward Future. (2026). Loop Library: Repeatable AI Agent Workflows. Read free
  5. Microsoft Research. (2023). AutoGen: multi-agent LLM applications. Available online — earlier multi-agent framing; included for history.
  6. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence, Ch. 4. [Provided]

Day 4 — AI for Urban Health Workflows

Required

  1. Mollick, E., & Mollick, L. (2024). Using AI to implement effective teaching strategies. Wharton. Available online
    • Human–AI collaboration frameworks that transfer to public-health workflows.
  2. Philadelphia Department of Public Health. (2024). Community Health Assessment. Available online — background for the scenarios.

Recommended

  1. The Lancet Public Health. (2025). Artificial intelligence in public health: promises, challenges, and an agenda. Available online — current field overview.
  2. Anthropic. (2025). Coding agents in the social sciences. [Provided] / Find it
  3. World Economic Forum. (2025). New Earth-observation tech is boosting climate intelligence. Find it — remote sensing for urban health.
  4. Lee, E. C., et al. (2023). AI for public health surveillance. Annual Review of Public Health, 44, 349–373. Find it
  5. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence, Ch. 5–6. [Provided]
