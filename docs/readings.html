Readings

Course Readings

This page lists all readings for the course, organized by day. Most readings link to their source or a search; [Find it] links resolve the exact title. Readings marked [Provided] will be made available through the course materials. All others are available online at the links provided.

Pre-Course Readings (Suggested)

These are optional but recommended to get the most out of the course:

  1. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI. Portfolio/Penguin. Chapters 1–2. [Provided]
    • An accessible introduction to how AI is changing work and why public health professionals need to engage with it.
  2. Topol, E. (2019). High-performance medicine: the convergence of human and artificial intelligence. Nature Medicine, 25(1), 44–56. Available online
    • A landmark review of AI applications in medicine and health, providing context for the course.
  3. World Health Organization. (2021). Ethics and governance of artificial intelligence for health. WHO. Available online
    • Read the executive summary (pp. 1–12) for an international perspective on AI ethics in health.

Day 1 Readings

Required

  1. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI. Chapters 1–2. [Provided]

  2. Anthropic. (2024). Prompt Engineering Guide. Available online

    • Practical guide to writing effective prompts. Focus on the “Be clear and direct” and “Use examples” sections.

Recommended

  1. Vaswani, A., et al. (2017). Attention is all you need. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 30. Available online
    • The original transformer paper. Read for historical context; the technical details are not required for this course.
  2. Governor Shapiro. (2024). Executive Order: Establishing governance and guardrails for Commonwealth use of artificial intelligence. Available online
    • Pennsylvania’s approach to AI governance in state agencies.

Day 2 Readings

Required

  1. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence. Chapter 3: “AI as a Creative.” [Provided]

  2. Jowsey, T., Corral Barrios, G., & Taggart, A. (2024). AI-assisted thematic analysis: the future of qualitative data analysis in health professions education research? Medical Education, 58(11), 1276–1278. Find it

    • Critical examination of using AI for qualitative data analysis. Key reading for Activity 4.

Recommended

  1. OpenDataPhilly. (2024). Heat Vulnerability Index — Metadata and Documentation. Available online

    • Review the dataset documentation before class to prepare for Activity 3.

  2. Meskó, B., & Topol, E. (2023). The imperative for regulatory oversight of large language models (or generative AI) in healthcare. npj Digital Medicine, 6(1), 120. Available online

  3. Gichoya, J. W., et al. (2022). AI recognition of patient race in medical imaging: a modelling study. The Lancet Digital Health, 4(6), e406–e414. Available online

    • Demonstrates how AI can identify race from medical images in ways humans cannot, raising concerns about automated bias.

Day 3 Readings

Required

  1. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence. Chapter 4: “AI as a Coworker.” [Provided]

  2. Anthropic. (2025). Claude Code Documentation. Available online

    • Overview of the Claude Code CLI tool. Skim the getting started section and capabilities overview.

Recommended

  1. Microsoft Research. (2023). AutoGen: Enabling next-gen LLM applications via multi-agent conversation. Available online
    • Technical paper on multi-agent AI systems. Read the introduction and discussion sections for context.
  2. Google. (2025). Google AI Studio Documentation. Available online
    • Getting started guide for Google AI Studio. Review before Activity 4.
  3. Ollama. (2025). Ollama Documentation. Available online
    • Review if you plan to install Ollama for Activity 6.

Day 4 Readings

Required

  1. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence. Chapters 5–6: “AI as an Expert” and “AI as a Coach.” [Provided]

  2. Mollick, E., & Mollick, L. (2024). Using AI to implement effective teaching strategies in classrooms: Five strategies, including prompts. The Wharton School Research Paper. Available online

    • While focused on education, the frameworks for AI-human collaboration are directly applicable to public health workflow design.

Recommended

  1. Philadelphia Department of Public Health. (2024). Community Health Assessment. Available online
    • Background reading for the scenario-based activities. Review the executive summary and key findings.
  2. Lee, E. C., et al. (2023). Artificial intelligence for public health surveillance: opportunities and challenges. Annual Review of Public Health, 44, 349–373. Find it

Day 5 Readings

Required

  1. Mollick, E. (2024). Co-Intelligence. Chapters 7–8. [Provided]

  2. Obermeyer, Z., Powers, B., Vogeli, C., & Mullainathan, S. (2019). Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of populations. Science, 366(6464), 447–453. Available online

    • Essential reading on algorithmic bias in healthcare. This is the real case discussed in Activity 1, Dilemma 4.

Recommended

  1. Mitchell, M., et al. (2019). Model cards for model reporting. Proceedings of the Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency, 220–229. Available online

    • The original model cards paper. Key reading on AI transparency.

  2. Gebru, T., et al. (2021). Datasheets for datasets. Communications of the ACM, 64(12), 86–92. Available online

    • Companion to the model cards paper, focused on documenting training data.

  3. Benjamin, R. (2019). Race After Technology: Abolitionist Tools for the New Jim Code. Polity Press. Publisher

    • Chapter 1: “Engineered Inequity.” [Provided excerpt]
    • Critical examination of how technology can reinforce racial inequality.

  4. Vayena, E., Blasimme, A., & Cohen, I. G. (2018). Machine learning in medicine: addressing ethical challenges. PLoS Medicine, 15(11), e1002689. Available online

  5. Char, D. S., Shah, N. H., & Magnus, D. (2018). Implementing machine learning in health care—addressing ethical challenges. New England Journal of Medicine, 378(11), 981–983. Available online

Additional Qualitative Research Readings

For participants interested in AI-assisted qualitative research:

  1. Jowsey, T., Corral Barrios, G., & Taggart, A. (2024). AI-assisted thematic analysis: the future of qualitative data analysis in health professions education research? Medical Education, 58(11), 1276–1278. Find it (Also listed under Day 2.)

  2. Dai, S. C., Stedman, A., Cheng, C. K., Chauhan, S., Liang, R., & Leung, F. H. (2024). Using large language models to generate qualitative research interview guides on patient experiences. JMIR Medical Education, 10, e59608. Available online

  3. Marathe, S., & Toyama, K. (2018). Semi-automated coding for qualitative research: A user-centered inquiry and initial prototypes. Proceedings of the 2018 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems, 1–12. Find it

  4. Tai, R. (2024). An assessment of the quality of qualitative research conducted using AI tools like ChatGPT. Preprint. Find it

Recommended Resources

Books

    • The primary course text. Accessible, practical, and grounded in research.
  • Benjamin, R. (2019). Race After Technology: Abolitionist Tools for the New Jim Code. Polity Press.

  • Topol, E. (2019). Deep Medicine: How Artificial Intelligence Can Make Healthcare Human Again. Basic Books.
    • Optimistic but thoughtful perspective on AI in healthcare.
