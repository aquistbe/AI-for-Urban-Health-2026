Welcome

Welcome to AI for Urban Health, a week-long intensive course offered through Drexel University’s Urban Health Collaborative (UHC) Summer Institute. This course is designed for public health professionals—particularly those working in urban settings—who want to understand, evaluate, and practically apply artificial intelligence tools in their daily work.

Over five days, you will move from foundational AI concepts to hands-on experience with cutting-edge tools, culminating in practical workflow integration and responsible use frameworks. No prior programming experience is required.

Pre-Course Checklist

Please complete the following before Day 1:

  • Create a free account at ChatGPT (OpenAI)
  • Create a free account at Claude (Anthropic)
  • Create a free account at Google AI Studio (Google)
  • Create a free account at Perplexity
  • Complete the pre-course survey (link TBD)
  • Review the pre-course readings (optional but recommended)
  • Bring a laptop to each session
  • Bring a writing sample or work document you would like AI help with (de-identified if it contains any sensitive data)
WarningImportant: Data Privacy

Throughout this course, we will use AI platforms that send data to external servers. Never enter protected health information (PHI), personally identifiable information (PII), or other sensitive data into any AI tool unless you are using a locally hosted model (covered in Day 3) or a platform with a signed Business Associate Agreement (BAA).

When working with real-world examples, always de-identify data first. We will discuss data privacy in detail throughout the course and dedicate significant time to it on Day 5.

What You Will Learn

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  1. Explain core AI concepts (machine learning, deep learning, foundation models) in accessible terms
  2. Use generative AI platforms effectively for writing, analysis, and research tasks
  3. Apply prompt engineering techniques to get better, more reproducible results
  4. Analyze data using AI-assisted tools, including tabular, image, and text data
  5. Evaluate when AI tools are appropriate—and when they are not—for public health applications
  6. Build simple AI agents and integrate AI into professional workflows
  7. Identify ethical concerns including bias, privacy, and accountability
  8. Develop a personal plan for responsible AI integration in your work
