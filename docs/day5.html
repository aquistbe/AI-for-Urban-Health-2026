Lecture: Responsible AI for Public Health

FAIR Data Principles and AI

The FAIR principles—Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable—originally developed for research data management, have direct relevance for AI in public health:

  • Findable: AI models and training data should be documented and discoverable
  • Accessible: Clear terms of use and access protocols
  • Interoperable: Models should work with standard data formats and tools
  • Reusable: Models and their outputs should be usable for new purposes with clear licensing

Model Cards and Datasheets

Two tools for AI transparency:

  • Model Cards (Mitchell et al., 2019): Standardized documentation for AI models that includes intended use, limitations, performance across demographic groups, and ethical considerations
  • Datasheets for Datasets (Gebru et al., 2021): Documentation for training datasets that describes how data was collected, from whom, for what purpose, and with what limitations
NoteWhy This Matters for Public Health

If you are using an AI model to inform a health decision—whether it is triaging patient complaints, prioritizing neighborhood interventions, or drafting public communications—you should be able to answer: What data was this model trained on? Does it perform equally well for all populations we serve? What are its known failure modes?

Sources of Bias in AI Systems

Bias can enter AI systems at every stage:

  1. Historical bias: Training data reflects past inequities (e.g., health records from a system that underserved Black patients)
  2. Representation bias: Some populations are overrepresented or underrepresented in training data
  3. Measurement bias: Proxies used for outcomes may not mean the same thing across groups (e.g., healthcare costs as a proxy for health need)
  4. Aggregation bias: Models that work well on average may fail for specific subgroups
  5. Deployment bias: Models used in contexts different from what they were designed for
  6. Feedback loops: Biased outputs influence future data collection, reinforcing the bias

Privacy: HIPAA, GDPR, and LLM-Specific Risks

Key privacy considerations when using AI in public health:

  • HIPAA: Protected Health Information (PHI) should never be entered into consumer AI tools without a Business Associate Agreement (BAA). Most free-tier AI tools explicitly state they may use your inputs for training.
  • GDPR: European data protection standards apply if you work with data from EU residents.
  • LLM-specific risks:
    • Training data inclusion: Your prompts may be used to train future models (check each platform’s data usage policy)
    • Memorization: LLMs can sometimes reproduce training data, including potentially sensitive information
    • Inference attacks: Seemingly anonymous prompts can reveal sensitive information in aggregate
    • Prompt injection: Malicious content can manipulate AI behavior

The Black Box Problem

Many AI systems—especially deep learning models—are difficult or impossible to fully explain. This creates challenges for:

  • Clinical decision support: Can you trust a recommendation you cannot explain?
  • Policy decisions: How do you justify an AI-informed policy to the public?
  • Legal accountability: Who is responsible when an opaque AI system makes an error?
  • Public trust: Will communities trust AI-informed health interventions?

Accountability

When AI assists with public health decisions, clear accountability is essential:

  • The human who acts on AI output bears responsibility for the consequences
  • Organizations need AI governance frameworks that define who can use AI, for what purposes, with what oversight
  • Documentation of AI use in decision-making processes should become standard practice
  • Audit mechanisms should be built in to review AI-assisted decisions

This maps onto the three inverse laws introduced on Day 1: non-anthropomorphism (the model has no judgment to defer to), non-deference (verify before acting), and non-abdication (you stay answerable for the result). Framing adapted from Beam & Aslim, “Thinking With Agents” (Univ. of Vermont, 2026).

Environmental Costs

AI has significant environmental costs that are often overlooked:

  • Training large language models requires enormous computational resources
  • A single ChatGPT query uses significantly more energy than a Google search
  • Data centers require cooling and consume large amounts of water
  • These costs are disproportionately borne by communities near data centers

Community Co-Design

Responsible AI in public health requires engaging the communities being studied or served:

  • Nothing about us without us: Communities should be involved in designing AI systems that affect them
  • Participatory design: Include community members in defining problems, selecting data, and evaluating outputs
  • Transparency: Be clear with communities about when and how AI is being used
  • Benefit sharing: Ensure that the benefits of AI-assisted public health reach the communities that contributed their data

Activity 1: Ethical Dilemma Scenarios

Activity 1: Ethical Dilemma Scenarios

Duration: ~45 minutes | Format: Small group discussion (15 min per dilemma, rotate through 2–3)

Goal: Grapple with realistic ethical challenges in AI-assisted public health.

Your group will discuss 2–3 of the following scenarios. For each:

  1. Identify the core ethical tensions
  2. Discuss what you would do and why
  3. Consider what policies or guardrails could prevent the problem
  4. Note areas of disagreement within your group

Dilemma 1: Hospital AI Chatbot Error

A Philadelphia hospital deploys an AI chatbot on its patient-facing website to help patients assess symptoms and determine whether they need emergency care. The chatbot correctly handles most cases, but tells a patient experiencing atypical heart attack symptoms (which are more common in women) to “rest and take over-the-counter pain medication.” The patient delays seeking care and suffers significant cardiac damage.

Discussion Questions:

  • Who is responsible—the hospital, the chatbot vendor, the physician who approved the system?
  • Should AI chatbots be used for symptom assessment at all? Under what conditions?
  • How should the system have handled uncertainty?
  • What testing should have been done before deployment?

Dilemma 2: Public Health Dashboard Failure

Your health department builds an AI-powered dashboard that predicts COVID-19 outbreaks by neighborhood, integrating wastewater surveillance, emergency department data, and social media activity. The model works well in wealthy neighborhoods with extensive data infrastructure but performs poorly in low-income communities with limited data sources, effectively creating a two-tiered surveillance system. Resources are allocated based on the dashboard’s predictions.

Discussion Questions:

  • Is it better to deploy an imperfect system that helps some neighborhoods, or wait for one that works equally well everywhere?
  • How does this scenario reflect broader patterns in health equity?
  • What data sources could improve performance in under-monitored neighborhoods?
  • How should you communicate the system’s limitations to decision-makers?

Dilemma 3: Mobile Location Data for Outbreak Tracking

During a syphilis outbreak concentrated in specific Philadelphia neighborhoods, an analyst proposes purchasing anonymized mobile phone location data to identify social networks and gathering places that might be linked to transmission. The data could help target testing and outreach. However, the neighborhoods most affected are predominantly communities of color with historical reasons to distrust public health surveillance.

Discussion Questions:

  • Does the public health benefit justify the surveillance implications?
  • What is the difference between “anonymized” and truly anonymous data?
  • How does the historical context (Tuskegee, other abuses) shape the ethical calculus?
  • What community engagement would be needed before proceeding?
  • What alternative approaches might achieve similar goals with less risk?

Dilemma 4: Optum Healthcare Algorithm Bias (Real Case)

A widely reported study (Obermeyer et al., 2019, Science) found that an algorithm used by Optum and many US health systems to identify patients who would benefit from “high-risk care management” programs was systematically biased against Black patients. The algorithm used healthcare costs as a proxy for healthcare needs. Because Black patients had historically received less healthcare spending (due to access barriers and systemic racism), the algorithm concluded they were healthier than equally sick White patients. At a given risk score, Black patients were significantly sicker than White patients.

Discussion Questions:

  • Why was using healthcare costs as a proxy for need problematic? What alternatives might work better?
  • How could this bias have been detected before deployment?
  • What does this case teach us about using historical data to train AI systems?
  • How should organizations audit AI systems for bias?

ImportantKey Takeaway

AI image generation reflects the biases present in its training data, which is largely sourced from the internet. These biases can reinforce stereotypes about who belongs in professional roles, what communities “look like,” and whose health matters. Critical evaluation of AI outputs is essential.

Activity 3: Privacy Quick Assessment

Activity 3: Privacy Quick Assessment

Duration: ~15 minutes | Format: Individual assessment

Goal: Develop practical skills for assessing privacy risks when using AI tools.

Data Inventory

Think about the data you work with regularly. For each type, assess the risk of entering it into an AI tool:

Data Type Example Risk Level Can Use AI? Mitigation
Aggregate statistics “Philadelphia has X rate of…” Low Yes, freely None needed
De-identified data Census tract-level health data Low–Medium Yes, with caution Verify de-identification
Identified but public Restaurant inspection records Medium Context-dependent Check data use agreements
PII without PHI Employee email addresses Medium–High Generally no De-identify first
PHI Patient records High No (unless BAA in place) Use local models only
Pre-publication research Unpublished study results Medium Caution Check funder/IRB policies

Re-identification Risk Assessment

Even “de-identified” data can sometimes be re-identified. Consider:

  • Small cell sizes (e.g., “1 case of rare disease in ZIP code 19104 in adults over 85”)
  • Unusual combinations of variables
  • Geographic specificity combined with demographic details

Philadelphia AI Policy Recap

Throughout this week, we have discussed AI governance in the context of Philadelphia and Pennsylvania:

  • Pennsylvania Executive Order: The state has established AI governance guidelines for state agencies
  • Philadelphia city policy: Currently in development; monitor for updates through the city’s Office of Innovation and Technology
  • Your role: As public health professionals, you can help shape responsible AI policy by:
    • Participating in policy development processes
    • Sharing your hands-on experience with AI tools
    • Advocating for health equity in AI governance
    • Building internal capacity and guidelines within your teams
