Lecture: AI Concepts and History

What Is Artificial Intelligence?

AI is a broad field encompassing any system that can perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence. We will walk through the key layers:

  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): The broadest category—machines that can sense, reason, act, and adapt
  • Machine Learning (ML): Systems that learn patterns from data rather than following explicit rules
    • Supervised learning: Learning from labeled examples (e.g., classifying chest X-rays)
    • Unsupervised learning: Finding patterns without labels (e.g., clustering neighborhoods by health outcomes)
    • Reinforcement learning: Learning through trial and reward (e.g., optimizing treatment protocols)
  • Deep Learning: ML using neural networks with many layers, enabling learning from raw data (images, text, audio)
  • Foundation Models / Large Language Models (LLMs): Models trained on massive datasets that can be adapted to many tasks

The Transformer Revolution

In 2017, the paper “Attention Is All You Need” introduced the transformer architecture, which enabled:

  • Processing entire sequences in parallel (not word by word)
  • The “attention mechanism”—allowing models to focus on relevant parts of the input
  • Scaling to billions of parameters, creating increasingly capable models

This architecture underlies ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and essentially all modern LLMs.

Why Urban Health Needs AI

Urban public health generates enormous amounts of data—surveillance records, community surveys, environmental monitoring, clinical data, policy documents—yet many departments are resource-constrained. AI can help with:

  • Rapid literature synthesis and evidence review
  • Data analysis and visualization with limited statistical staff
  • Drafting reports, communications, and policy briefs
  • Identifying patterns in large datasets
  • Translating materials for diverse communities
  • Streamlining routine administrative tasks
TipKey Concept: AI as a Tool, Not a Replacement

AI is most effective when it augments human expertise rather than replacing it. Throughout this course, we will focus on human-in-the-loop approaches where AI assists with tasks but humans make the final decisions.

A Working Vocabulary

A few terms you will hear all week: a model is the trained system itself (Claude, GPT, Gemini) — a pattern-completion engine, not a database; a token is a chunk of text (~¾ of a word) counted for usage and limits; a prompt is what you send, including any context; the context window is how much it can hold in mind at once; a session is one conversation (no memory across sessions unless you build it); and an agent is a model given tools and a goal that it pursues in steps (Day 3).

Three Ways to Work With AI

The same model behaves differently depending on where you meet it: in a chat window (browser; ask, paste, generate; can’t see your files), inside your tools (an editor or app that sees and edits your work in place), or agentically (you give a goal and it plans and runs multi-step work). The move from “ask and copy” to “delegate and verify” is the biggest shift this year.

The Three Inverse Laws of AI

Asimov’s laws constrain robots to protect people; these “inverse laws” constrain us: non-anthropomorphism (don’t attribute judgment or intent to the model), non-deference (never treat output as authoritative without verification), and non-abdication (you remain responsible for the result — “the AI told me so” is never a defense). We return to these on Day 5.

Vocabulary, the three modes, and the inverse laws are adapted from Beam & Aslim, “Thinking With Agents” (Univ. of Vermont, 2026).

Activity 1: Explore Generative AI Platforms

Duration: ~40 minutes | Format: Individual exploration + group discussion

Goal: Get hands-on experience with multiple AI platforms and understand their strengths and differences.

Instructions

  1. Open each of the following platforms (you should have created accounts before today):
    • ChatGPT (OpenAI)
    • Claude (Anthropic)
    • Gemini (Google)
    • Perplexity
  2. Ask each platform the same prompt and compare responses:

Prompt 1 (Factual):

What are the leading causes of premature mortality in Philadelphia, and how do they differ by neighborhood?

Prompt 2 (Analytical):

I work at a city health department. Summarize the evidence on heat-related illness prevention strategies that have been effective in urban areas similar to Philadelphia.

Prompt 3 (Creative):

Draft three social media posts (for X/Twitter) promoting free lead testing for children in North Philadelphia. The tone should be warm, urgent but not alarming, and culturally appropriate.

  1. Compare and document your findings using this table:
Feature ChatGPT Claude Gemini Perplexity

Length/detail

Discussion Questions

  • Which platform gave the most accurate response? How did you verify?
  • Which platform cited its sources most clearly?
  • Did any platform refuse to answer or add caveats? Why might that be?
  • Which would you reach for first for different types of tasks?
TipPlatform Differences to Watch For
  • ChatGPT has a large ecosystem of plugins and custom GPTs; strong at creative and conversational tasks
  • Claude tends toward longer, more nuanced responses with explicit reasoning; strong safety guardrails
  • Gemini integrates with Google Workspace and has strong multimodal capabilities
  • Perplexity is purpose-built for research with inline citations from web sources

Activity 2: Prompt Engineering Practice

Activity 2: Prompt Engineering Practice

Duration: ~40 minutes | Format: Guided practice with partner discussion

Goal: Learn and practice the PTCF framework for writing effective prompts.

The PTCF Framework

Good prompts have four components:

Component Description Example
Persona Who should the AI act as? “You are a public health epidemiologist…”
Task What specifically should it do? “Write a 500-word executive summary…”
Context What background information is needed? “This is for the city council health committee…”
Format How should the output be structured? “Use bullet points with headers, include a recommendation section…”

Practice Prompts

Try these prompts using the PTCF framework. Start with the “basic” version, then improve it:

Scenario 1: Herd Immunity Explanation

Basic prompt: “Explain herd immunity.”

Improved prompt (try writing your own first, then compare):

Persona: You are a community health educator working in a diverse urban neighborhood in Philadelphia.

Task: Explain the concept of herd immunity to a group of parents at a community health fair.

Context: Many parents in this community have questions about childhood vaccinations. Reading levels vary widely. Some community members have expressed vaccine hesitancy based on misinformation on social media.

Format: Write a 200-word explanation at an 8th-grade reading level. Use an analogy. End with one clear call to action. Avoid medical jargon.

Scenario 2: Vaccination Social Media Campaign

Basic prompt: “Write a social media post about flu shots.”

Your improved PTCF version: Write a prompt that specifies the audience (e.g., elderly residents in South Philadelphia), the platform (e.g., Facebook), the tone, and the desired call to action.

Scenario 3: Air Quality Executive Summary

Basic prompt: “Summarize air quality data.”

Your improved PTCF version: Specify who you are (e.g., environmental health analyst), what data you are summarizing (e.g., PM2.5 readings from 2024-2025 across Philadelphia neighborhoods), who the audience is (e.g., City Council), and what format the summary should take (e.g., 1-page brief with key findings, a table, and policy recommendations).

Advanced Techniques

After practicing the basics, try these:

  • Chain-of-thought prompting: Add “Think through this step by step” to complex analytical questions
  • Few-shot prompting: Give 1–2 examples of the output you want before asking for a new one
  • Structured output: Ask for JSON, CSV, or specific table formats
  • Iterative refinement: Start with a draft prompt, review the output, and revise the prompt
NoteReproducibility Note

AI outputs are not perfectly reproducible—the same prompt can generate different responses each time. For work that requires consistency, save your prompts, document which model and version you used, and note the date. Some platforms offer a “temperature” setting that affects randomness.

Activity 3: AI for Professional Writing

Activity 3: AI for Professional Writing

Duration: ~30 minutes | Format: Individual work + pair sharing

Goal: Practice using AI as a writing assistant for real public health work products.

Instructions

Choose one of the following tasks (or bring your own de-identified writing task):

Option A: Draft a Public Health Report Section

Ask AI to help you draft a section of a report on a topic relevant to your work. For example:

You are a public health analyst at the Philadelphia Department of Public Health. Draft the “Background” section (300–400 words) for a report on childhood asthma rates in Philadelphia. Include relevant national and local statistics, known risk factors in urban environments, and health equity considerations. Cite sources where possible and flag any statistics that should be verified.

Option B: Edit and Improve Existing Writing

Paste a paragraph from a real (de-identified) work document and ask the AI to:

  • Improve clarity and readability
  • Adjust the tone for a specific audience
  • Identify gaps in the argument
  • Suggest stronger transitions

Option C: Write a Policy Brief

Draft a 1-page policy brief recommending that Philadelphia expand its network of cooling centers based on heat vulnerability data. Include: an executive summary (2–3 sentences), the problem statement, key evidence, and 3 specific recommendations. The audience is City Council members who are not public health experts.

Pair Discussion

After completing your writing task, share with a partner:

  1. What prompt did you use? How did you refine it?
  2. What did the AI do well? What needed human editing?
  3. Would you use this approach in your actual work? Why or why not?
WarningReminder: De-Identify Your Data

If you bring a real work document to this activity, make sure it contains no protected health information (PHI), personally identifiable information (PII), or confidential agency data. When in doubt, create a fictional scenario based on your real work.
