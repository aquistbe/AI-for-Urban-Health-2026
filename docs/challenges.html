Bonus Challenge Exercises

Optional deep-dive exercises for advanced students

Intermediate to Expert | 45–90 minutes each

About These Exercises

These are optional stretch activities for students who finish core activities early or want to go deeper. They are more open-ended and technically demanding than the daily activities, and most need 45–90 minutes of focused work. They make excellent portfolio pieces.

Each exercise follows the same layout so it is easy to follow:

  • Goal — the one-sentence outcome.
  • Why it matters — why the skill is worth building.
  • Steps — numbered, concrete actions, with ready-to-paste example prompts you can adapt.
  • Hints — tips and common pitfalls.
  • Done when — a checklist so you know you have finished.
  • Deliverable — what to save or hand in.
  • Reflection — questions to deepen your thinking.
TipHow to use this page

Each exercise below is collapsed — click its title to expand it. Scan the index table, pick one that matches your skill level and interest, and jump to it (the link expands it for you). You do not need to do them in order. Working with qualitative data? Try Exercise 5. Comfortable with code? Try 3, 6, or 10.

Day 1 Challenges

Exercise 1 — Cross-Platform AI Benchmark

Day 1 · Intermediate · ~45 min · Tools: Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity

Goal: Compare how four AI platforms handle the same public-health task, and decide which to use for which kind of work.

Why it matters: You will inevitably choose among AI platforms for different tasks. A structured benchmark lets you decide from evidence rather than anecdote.

Steps

  1. Build a scoring rubric. Rate each response 1–5 on six dimensions:

    • Accuracy (facts and statistics are verifiable), Completeness (covers all parts), Actionability (specific enough to act on), Appropriate uncertainty (acknowledges limits), Source quality (cites real, relevant sources), Public-health framing (uses sound epidemiological concepts).

  2. Run the same prompt on all four (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity). Paste this exactly:

    You are advising the Philadelphia Department of Public Health. A cluster of 47 cases of acute gastrointestinal illness has been reported in 72 hours across three ZIP codes in South Philadelphia. Preliminary interviews suggest a common food source. Design a rapid investigation plan covering: (1) immediate actions in the first 24 hours, (2) epidemiological investigation steps, (3) laboratory testing priorities, (4) communication strategy for the public and providers, and (5) criteria for escalating to state and federal authorities.

  3. Record the exact prompt, date/time, and model version for each, and save each full response.

  4. Score and analyze. Fill in the rubric and write a ~400–500 word comparison: which gave the most operationally useful plan; which handled uncertainty best; did any hallucinate statistics, cite non-existent guidelines, or make unsafe recommendations; and which you would recommend for which task.

Hints

  • Open all four in separate tabs and paste the identical prompt to avoid wording drift.
  • Watch specifically for invented citations and fabricated local statistics — the most common, most dangerous failure.

Done when

Deliverable: A document with your completed rubric, a summary table, and the comparison report.

Reflection: If you ran this next month, would the results hold? What does that imply about relying on platform comparisons? How would your rubric change for a clinical vs. an epidemiological audience?

Exercise 2 — Structured Output Engineering

Day 1 · Advanced · ~60 min · Tools: Claude or ChatGPT, a text editor

Goal: Build prompts that reliably turn messy public-health text into consistent, machine-readable JSON.

Why it matters: Public-health work constantly involves pulling structured facts out of unstructured text — case reports, surveillance summaries, meeting notes. Reliable structured output is a high-value, reusable skill.

Steps

  1. Work from this report excerpt:

    Between January and March 2026, the Philadelphia Department of Public Health received 23 reports of elevated blood lead levels (BLLs above 5 mcg/dL) in children under 6 in the Kensington neighborhood. Of these, 8 had BLLs of 5–9 mcg/dL, 12 of 10–19 mcg/dL, and 3 of 20 mcg/dL or above. Affected children ranged from 8 months to 5 years old. Home inspections found deteriorating lead paint in 18 homes, 14 built before 1950. The Department issued abatement orders for all confirmed properties and referred families to the lead poisoning prevention program.

  2. Design a JSON schema that captures every key data point (counts, ranges, ages, housing facts, actions). Then write an extraction prompt, for example:

    Extract the data from the report below into JSON matching exactly this schema: {schema}. Use null for anything not stated. Return only valid JSON, no commentary.

    Test and refine until the output is valid, parseable JSON.

  3. Build a 3-step chain: (1) extract raw text → JSON; (2) enrich — add CDC blood-lead action-level classifications, relevant Philadelphia housing rules, and national benchmarks; (3) generate — turn the enriched JSON into a formatted departmental briefing.

  4. Stress test with a second, different report (ask the AI to generate one on another topic). Note which parts of your prompts generalize and which are topic-specific; revise for robustness.

Hints

  • Ask for “only valid JSON, no prose” — stray commentary is the usual reason parsing breaks.
  • Validate each step’s output is usable as the next step’s input before chaining further.

Done when

Deliverable: Your schema, the full text of each prompt, the actual outputs at each step, and notes on errors and fixes.

Reflection: What was hardest for the AI to extract accurately? How would you validate AI extraction against the source text in a production workflow?

Day 2 Challenges

Exercise 3 — Multi-Source Data Integration with AI

Day 2 · Advanced · ~90 min · Tools: Claude or ChatGPT; R, Python, or Stata

Goal: Link environmental, health, and infrastructure datasets into one analysis with AI assistance.

Why it matters: Real public-health analysis rarely uses a single dataset. Joining across sources is essential for understanding health disparities — and the hard part is usually reconciling mismatched geographies.

Steps

  1. Get the data. Use the provided Heat Vulnerability sample CSV (census-tract level), or download the real Heat Vulnerability by Census Tract plus 311 Service Requests (filter to environmental/housing, last 12 months) and one dataset of your choice (demographics, crashes, tree canopy) from OpenDataPhilly.

  2. Examine each and document the geographic unit (tract, ZIP, point) and possible join keys.

  3. Have the AI plan the join. Share the data dictionaries and ask:

    These datasets use different geographic units ({list them}). Recommend the best strategy to join them, write code to harmonize the geographic identifiers, handle missing data, and add data-quality checks (record counts before/after the merge, key-variable distributions). Use {R / Python / Stata}.

    Run it, and document every change you made to the AI’s code and why.

  4. Analyze across datasets — e.g., correlation between HVI and 311 complaint density, or clusters where multiple risks converge. Produce at least two visuals (one map, one chart). Have the AI draft an interpretation, then check it for accuracy and appropriate causal language.

Hints

  • Converting between geographies (e.g., tract ↔︎ ZIP) loses information — ask the AI to state its crosswalk assumptions explicitly.
  • Always compare record counts before and after a merge; silent row loss is the classic join bug.

Done when

Deliverable: A reproducible document (R Markdown, Jupyter notebook, or Stata .do) with acquisition, merge code, visuals, and a short narrative.

Reflection: What was lost converting between geographic units? Where did the AI make incorrect assumptions? What dataset would strengthen this?

Exercise 4 — Reproducing a Published Epidemiologic Analysis

Day 2 · Expert · ~90 min · Tools: Claude or ChatGPT, Perplexity; R, Python, or Stata

Goal: Use AI to reproduce a published analysis from open data — and surface where it helps and where it introduces errors.

Why it matters: Reproducibility is core to rigor. AI can speed it up but also introduces subtle code errors you have to catch.

Steps

  1. Pick a study that: was published in the last 5 years, uses public data (BRFSS, NHANES, CDC WONDER, Census/ACS), reports at least one regression, and is relevant to urban health. Record the citation, DOI, and data source.

  2. Have the AI parse the methods. Paste the methods section and ask:

    From this methods section, identify the exact dataset, variables, inclusion/exclusion criteria, and statistical models. Then write {R / Python / Stata} code to download the data and reproduce the main analysis.

  3. Run and troubleshoot — expect to iterate. Document every change. Build a comparison table: variable/parameter, published result, your result, difference.

  4. Write up (500–700 words): what reproduced, what differed and why, what methodological details were missing from the paper, and how much time AI saved.

Hints

  • Start with the single headline result, not the whole paper — get one number to match first.
  • Differences often come from undocumented data-cleaning choices; note them as findings, not failures.

Done when

Deliverable: Citation, annotated code, results comparison table, and the written report.

Reflection: What does this reveal about reproducibility in the field? Could AI-assisted reproduction become part of peer review?

Exercise 5 — Advanced Qualitative Coding Challenge

Day 2 · Advanced · ~60 min · Tools: Claude or ChatGPT, a spreadsheet

Goal: Develop a codebook, apply it, and measure how well AI coding agrees with your own.

Why it matters: AI-assisted qualitative coding raises real questions about reliability, validity, and whether AI captures the nuance an experienced researcher brings.

Steps

  1. Get transcript text. Use the provided climate focus-group transcript, or these short excerpts on heat-safety barriers:

    Speaker 1: “When it gets really hot, the elderly folks in my area just stay inside with the windows closed. They are afraid to use the AC because of the bill. One lady told me she’d rather sweat than get a $300 bill.”

    Speaker 2: “Transportation is huge. Even if there’s a cooling center, how do you get there if you don’t drive? The bus routes in North Philly don’t go near them.”

    Speaker 3: “Communication is a problem. Alerts go out in English, but half my clients speak Spanish or Vietnamese, and ‘excessive heat warning’ means nothing to them.”

  2. Develop a codebook. Ask the AI for an inductive codebook, then review and finalize 8–12 codes with clear definitions.

  3. Apply it two ways. Code additional excerpts (ask the AI to generate realistic continuations) with the AI, and code the same excerpts yourself independently. Put both in a spreadsheet coding matrix.

  4. Measure agreement. Calculate Cohen’s kappa or percent agreement between you and the AI.

  5. Write a 200–300 word methods paragraph describing the AI-assisted approach, reliability assessment, and limitations.

Hints

  • Lock the codebook before coding the comparison excerpts, or your kappa is meaningless.
  • Look hardest at where you and the AI disagree — that’s where the method’s limits show.

Done when

Deliverable: Codebook, coded transcripts, reliability statistics, and methods paragraph.

Reflection: At what agreement level would you trust AI as a primary coder? What are the IRB implications of using AI on identifiable qualitative data?

Exercise 6 — Building an Automated Data Pipeline

Day 3 · Expert · ~90 min · Tools: Claude Code, Python, OpenDataPhilly API

Goal: Use an agentic CLI to build a working data pipeline that downloads, cleans, analyzes, and reports — on its own.

Why it matters: Departments repeat the same data chores constantly. Automating them frees analyst time for interpretation and action.

Steps

  1. Scope the pipeline. It should: (a) acquire 311 service requests (last 90 days, environmental/housing) from the OpenDataPhilly API; (b) clean — handle missing values, standardize addresses, validate dates, drop duplicates, geocode to census tract; (c) analyze — complaint count and per-capita rate by tract, most common type, median response time, week-over-week trend; (d) generate an HTML report with a table, bar chart, time series, and narrative.

  2. Have Claude Code build it. In a project folder, run claude and ask:

    Build a Python pipeline that {paste the four steps above}. Use clear functions, add logging and error handling, and make the date range and complaint filter configurable. Show me the script before running anything.

  3. Review before running. Check API endpoints, error handling, logging, and parameters. Then run and debug.

  4. Add two improvements — e.g., retry logic, response caching, or extra validation checks.

  5. Document (400–500 words): architecture, dependencies, error handling, maintenance, limitations.

Hints

  • Approve Claude Code’s file writes and commands one at a time the first run, so you see exactly what it does.
  • Cache the API response while developing so you are not re-downloading on every test run.

Done when

Deliverable: The working pipeline, the generated HTML report, and the technical documentation.

Reflection: What was the ratio of AI generation time to your debugging time? What are the risks of deploying AI-generated pipelines without thorough review?

Exercise 7 — Custom MCP Server Design

Day 3 · Expert · ~60 min · Tools: A text editor

Goal: Design (on paper) an MCP server that safely connects an AI agent to a public-health data system.

Why it matters: MCP lets AI assistants reach external data through a standard interface. Designing one forces careful thinking about access, security, and appropriate automation.

Steps

  1. Choose a scenario: Disease Surveillance Assistant, Community Health Assessment Tool, or Environmental Health Inspector Support.

  2. Specify 6–10 tools. For each, define name, description, parameters (with types), return structure, data source, and access control. For example:

    get_case_counts(disease: str, start_date: date, end_date: date, geography: str) → {counts by area} — reads the reportable-disease database; read-only; aggregate counts only, never individual records.

  3. Define security constraints: what the agent must never access, what requires human confirmation, how it handles requests for protected health information (PHI), and what audit logging is required.

  4. Write the agent’s system prompt (500–800 words): role and expertise, rules for tool use, data-interpretation guidance, explicit restrictions, and edge-case handling.

  5. Compare capabilities: a table of tasks the agent could do vs. how they are done today, with rough time savings.

Hints

  • Default every tool to read-only and aggregate; make any write or record-level access require explicit human confirmation.
  • Write the restrictions (“never do X”) as concretely as the capabilities — vague limits are where agents get into trouble.

Done when

Deliverable: A 3–5 page design document: use case, MCP spec, security constraints, system prompt, capability comparison.

Reflection: What governance would need to exist before deploying an agent with access to real public-health data systems?

Exercise 8 — Local vs. Cloud Model Evaluation

Day 3 · Intermediate · ~45 min · Tools: Ollama (gemma4:12b or qwen3:8b), Claude or ChatGPT

Goal: Measure the real quality gap between a local model and a cloud model, so you know when privacy is worth the tradeoff.

Why it matters: Knowing where local models are “good enough” lets you decide when data privacy justifies reduced capability.

Steps

  1. Write 10 test prompts — two each in: factual knowledge, data interpretation (give a small table), writing/summarization, multi-step reasoning, and code generation (working R or Python).
  2. Run all 10 on both a local model in Ollama and your cloud model, using identical text. Score each 1–5 on accuracy, completeness, relevance, and safety. Record response times.
  3. Summarize and recommend. Build a table, compute the average score gap by category, and write a 300–400 word memo: where local is adequate, where cloud is clearly better, and when staff should default to each.

Hints

  • Pull the local model ahead of time (ollama pull gemma4:12b); first load is slow.
  • The gap is usually smallest on summarization and largest on multi-step reasoning and code — check whether your results agree.

Done when

Deliverable: The 10 prompts, scoring table, summary analysis, and recommendation memo.

Reflection: How might results change with a larger local model (e.g., a 30B+ on a high-RAM machine)? Are there tasks where local is preferable beyond privacy?

Exercise 9 — AI-Assisted Grant Proposal Section

Day 3 · Advanced · ~75 min · Tools: Claude or ChatGPT, Perplexity

Goal: Draft a Specific Aims page and Research Strategy section using AI iteratively — as a drafting partner, not a one-shot generator.

Why it matters: Grant writing is time-intensive. AI helps a lot, but only when used iteratively with your expertise in the loop.

Steps

  1. Pick a topic at the intersection of AI and urban health relevant to Philadelphia. Use Perplexity to find 5–8 key references and state the literature gap.

  2. Draft Specific Aims iteratively:

    Draft a 1-page NIH Specific Aims for a study on {topic}: an opening paragraph establishing the problem, an overarching goal, and three aims, each with a short rationale and a hypothesis.

    Then revise at least three elements with your own field knowledge, and track your edits.

  3. Draft a 2-page Significance/Innovation the same way. For each paragraph, label it: AI-generated with minor edits, substantially rewritten, or human-written.

Hints

  • Feed the AI your references and field knowledge first — ungrounded aims read generic and cite phantom literature.
  • Keep a prompt log; you will reuse the good prompts on your next proposal.

Done when

Deliverable: Specific Aims, Significance/Innovation, a prompt log, and a short reflection.

Reflection: Where was AI most and least helpful? How would you disclose AI use in a grant submission (check current NIH policy)?

Exercise 13 — Create a Skill + a Scheduled Task

Day 3 · Intermediate · ~45 min · Tools: Claude (claude.ai / Claude Desktop / Cowork) or Claude Code

Goal: Package one of your recurring tasks as a reusable Skill, then (optionally) wire it into a scheduled task that runs on its own.

Why it matters: A Skill captures your way of doing a task so the agent repeats it consistently across conversations and tools — and a scheduled task turns a one-off into standing automation. Together they move you from “ask each time” to “set it up once.”

Steps

  1. Pick a recurring task with a consistent structure — e.g., “format a weekly surveillance summary,” “de-identify a dataset to our standard,” or “turn meeting notes into an action list.”

  2. Author a SKILL.md. Create a folder with a SKILL.md that has YAML frontmatter and instructions:

    ---
name: weekly-surveillance-summary
description: Format raw weekly case counts into our standard departmental summary. Use when the user provides weekly surveillance numbers.
---
# Weekly Surveillance Summary
Steps:
1. Produce a one-paragraph plain-language summary.
2. Add a table: indicator, this week, last week, % change.
3. Flag any indicator up >20% week-over-week.
Tone: factual, no alarm. Never invent numbers not provided.

    Add optional references/ (a template) or scripts/ (a helper script) if useful. Shortcut: use the Skill Creator (built into Claude Desktop/Cowork; /plugin install skill-creator in Claude Code) to generate and refine it for you.

  3. Install and test it. Upload the zipped folder on claude.ai (Settings → Customize → Skills), or point Claude Code/Cowork at it. Start a fresh conversation, give it a realistic input, and confirm the skill triggers and produces your format. If it doesn’t trigger, sharpen the description (it controls when the skill fires) and retest.

  4. (Optional) Schedule it. In Cowork (desktop, paid plan), turn the task into a recurring task: describe it once, pick a cadence (e.g., every Monday), and confirm each run can reach your files/skills. Schedule only work that’s safe to run with a human reviewing the output afterward.

Hints

  • The description is the most important line — it decides when the skill activates. Be specific about the trigger.
  • De-identify any real data before it goes into the model, and bake that into the skill’s instructions.
  • Keep SKILL.md in version control so you can track changes over time.

Done when

Deliverable: Your skill folder, one worked example, and a note on which tasks are good vs. poor candidates for a skill or a schedule.

NoteFree-tier note

Authoring a SKILL.md is free for anyone (it’s just a folder). Using skills in claude.ai and Cowork scheduled tasks generally require a paid plan and the desktop app — if you don’t have access, the instructor will demo this part.

Reflection: Which of your tasks are good candidates for a skill, and which should never be automated on a schedule without review? What could go wrong if a scheduled task runs on stale or bad input?

Exercise 10 — Build an Interactive Public Health Dashboard

Day 4 · Advanced · ~90 min · Tools: Claude Code or ChatGPT, HVI data, a web browser

Goal: Turn heat-vulnerability data into a self-contained interactive dashboard — and design the story it tells.

Why it matters: Dashboards let stakeholders explore data themselves. AI can write the code; your job is the design and the narrative.

Steps

  1. Plan. Decide the audience, the 3–5 questions the dashboard answers, and which variables to show. Sketch a wireframe and write a 150–200 word design brief.

  2. Build. Use the Heat Vulnerability sample CSV. Share your brief and data and ask:

    Build a single self-contained HTML file (inline CSS/JS, no external dependencies except a CDN charting library) from this CSV: 3–4 KPI cards, a histogram of HVI scores, a scatterplot of two components, one more visualization (map or comparison), at least one dropdown filter, and a short narrative. Make it colorblind-friendly and clearly labeled.

    Iterate to fix issues and improve styling.

  3. Review. Can a non-technical viewer get the takeaway in 30 seconds? Are charts labeled, colors accessible, and the narrative contextual?

Hints

  • Ask for one self-contained .html file so it opens with a double-click and is easy to share.
  • Give the AI a few real rows of the CSV so it builds the parser around the actual columns.

Done when

Deliverable: The self-contained HTML dashboard, plus your design brief and wireframe.

Reflection: How would you adapt this for a community vs. a technical audience? What data-quality caveats should travel with the dashboard?

Exercise 12 — Personal AI Workflow Automation

Day 4 · Intermediate · ~60 min · Tools: Claude or ChatGPT, tools from your daily work

Goal: Turn one recurring task from your real job into a reusable, quality-checked AI workflow.

Why it matters: The highest-impact AI integration targets specific, repeated, structured tasks — not vague “use AI more.”

Steps

  1. Pick a task. List 3–5 recurring tasks with frequency, duration, input type, and whether output is internal or public. Choose the best candidate (high time, high repetition, low risk).
  2. Build a workflow package:
    • a reusable prompt template (PTCF structure) with placeholders for variable inputs;
    • a pre-processing checklist (de-identify, format, gather context);
    • a quality checklist of 5–8 checks (accuracy, completeness, tone, safety);
    • one fully worked example (filled prompt → AI output → final after your review).
  3. Test and validate on three real, de-identified examples. Record time with vs. without the workflow, quality, and any failures. Estimate annual time saved.

Hints

  • De-identify inputs before they ever reach the model — bake that into the pre-processing checklist.
  • A workflow a colleague can run from your template is far more valuable than a one-off prompt.

Done when

Deliverable: Prompt template, checklists, three tested examples with timing, and the savings estimate.

Reflection: What training would a new colleague need to use your workflow? How would you monitor its quality over time?

Exercise 11 — Adversarial AI Testing for Public Health Safety

Day 5 · Advanced · ~60 min · Tools: Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini

Goal: Systematically probe AI for dangerous, biased, or misleading outputs before anyone would rely on it.

Why it matters: Before deploying AI in public-health settings you need to know how it fails. Adversarial testing finds the failures on purpose, in a safe setting.

Steps

  1. Design a test protocol — two prompts in each of five failure categories (10 total):
    • Inappropriate clinical advice (specific recommendations the AI shouldn’t give), Biased recommendations (does advice change by race, neighborhood, or income?), Statistical hallucination (asks for local stats that may not exist), Privacy violations (tries to get identifying information), Overconfidence (does it communicate uncertainty?).
  2. Execute each prompt on at least two platforms. For each, record the exact prompt, the full response, pass/fail, severity (low/medium/high/critical), and the guardrail that would prevent the failure.
  3. Write a 1-page safety assessment: executive summary; a risk matrix (categories × platforms, with severity); 3–5 key findings with examples; mitigation recommendations; and a deployment recommendation (deploy / deploy with conditions / do not deploy).

Hints

  • Keep these tests in a sandbox — the point is to surface failures, not to act on any output.
  • Severity matters more than pass-rate: one critical failure outweighs many minor ones.

Done when

Deliverable: The test protocol, documented results, and the 1-page safety assessment.

Reflection: How comprehensive was your testing? What might you have missed? Should safety testing be done by the team that will use the AI, or by an independent group?

This exercise pairs with the “three inverse laws” from Day 1 — especially non-deference (verify before trusting) and non-abdication (you stay responsible).
