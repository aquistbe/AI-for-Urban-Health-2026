Part 1 — Opening and lived experience

FACILITATOR: Thanks, everyone, for coming out tonight. We want to understand how the changing climate is affecting your health and your family's health, and then we'll talk through some ways the city and community groups might respond. There are no wrong answers — we want your honest experience. Let's start broad. When you think about the weather and your health over the last few summers, what comes to mind?

DENISE: Honestly? The heat. It's not the same as when I was coming up. My block is all brick and concrete, no trees to speak of. By July my house is like an oven upstairs. I've got high blood pressure and my doctor told me the heat makes it worse. There were nights last August I just couldn't sleep.

MARCUS: Same for me, but it's my daughter I worry about. She's got asthma. The bad air days — they call them code orange or code red — she can't really play outside. Last summer she ended up in the ER twice. The doctor said it was the heat plus the air quality together.

ROSA: For us it's the flooding. We're near the creek. Twice in three years the basement filled up. The second time, the mold came back and my youngest started coughing and didn't stop for weeks. We can't afford to keep ripping out drywall. I didn't even connect it to climate at first — I just thought we had bad luck. But the rain comes harder now, all at once.

TUAN: I drive for a delivery company, so I'm outside in it all day. The heat is brutal. I've felt dizzy, my heart racing. I keep going because I need the hours, but I know it's not good for me. I worry one day I'll just pass out in the truck.

GLORIA: I'll say something nobody likes to talk about — what it does to your head. When it's that hot for that long, people get short. Tempers flare. My grandson gets anxious. And the older folks on my street, some of them just stay shut in with the windows closed because they're scared of falling or scared of the cost of the AC. They get isolated. That's a health thing too, even if it doesn't look like one.

JAMES: I'm a senior, on a fixed income. For me it comes down to money. You want me to run the AC? My electric bill in August was almost double. So I sit in front of a fan and tell myself I'm fine. I had a spell last year — got real lightheaded, confused. My neighbor found me. I don't tell my kids because I don't want them worrying.

AALIYAH: I'm younger than most folks here, and I'll be honest, a lot of my friends feel like — what's the point? Like the planet's already cooked and nobody in charge is doing anything. It's a heavy feeling. So when you ask about health, I think mental health for my generation. Climate anxiety is real, even if older people roll their eyes at it.

FACILITATOR: Thank you all — that's a lot of honesty and I appreciate it. I'm hearing heat, air quality and asthma, flooding and mold, the strain on people working outside, the cost of staying cool, and the emotional weight of all of it. Did I miss anything?

DENISE: The food. When my budget goes to the electric bill, it comes out of groceries. And the corner store, when it's that hot, half the produce is already turning. So we eat worse in the summer, which for a diabetic like me is a problem.

ROSA: And the mosquitoes. With the standing water after the floods, they're everywhere now. My neighbor said there were warnings about West Nile. We never used to think about that here.

Part 2 — What's working, what's missing

FACILITATOR: Let's talk about responses. When the heat or the air gets bad, what do you do now? And what do you wish existed?

MARCUS: I check the air quality on my phone before I let my daughter out. But honestly I found that on my own. Nobody told me to. The school doesn't send anything home. I think a lot of parents just don't know.

GLORIA: There are cooling centers — the rec center, the library. But here's the thing. People my mother's age aren't going to take two buses in 98-degree heat to go sit in a cooling center. By the time you get there you're already sick. They need to be close, or somebody needs to bring people there.

JAMES: And nobody knows where they are half the time. I couldn't tell you the hours. Is it open on the weekend? On a Sunday, when it's worst? I don't know.

DENISE: What I want is help with my actual house. Trees on the block. A real fix, not just "go somewhere with AC for the afternoon and then come back to your oven." Some neighborhoods have all the tree cover. Drive ten minutes and it's like a different world. Why not us?

TUAN: For people who work outside, I'd want my employer to have rules. Water breaks, shade, slow down when it's a red day. Right now it's all on me to decide whether I can keep going, and the truth is I can't afford to stop.

ROSA: Help with the flooding. Even just telling us honestly that this is going to keep happening, so we can plan. And help with the mold cleanup so my kids aren't breathing it. I'd rather have the truth than be surprised every year.

AALIYAH: I want to actually do something, not just be told to be scared. If there was a way for young people to plant trees, check on elders, help at the cooling centers — give us something real. That helps the anxiety more than any pamphlet.

FACILITATOR: That's helpful. I'm hearing a few things: people don't always know what's available or where; the existing options sometimes don't reach the people who need them most; and several of you want longer-term fixes — trees, home repairs, workplace protections — not just emergency stopgaps. And Aaliyah, this idea of giving people, especially young people, a role to play.

Part 3 — Testing the messages

FACILITATOR: Now I want to try something. We're working on how to communicate about all of this, and I have a few different ways of saying similar things. I'll read each one, and I want your gut reaction — does it grab you, does it turn you off, would you act on it? Be blunt. Here's the first.

Message A (climate framing): "Climate change is making our summers more dangerous. Take action to protect our community from extreme heat."

JAMES: "Climate change" — soon as I hear that, I think it's politics. Some folks on my block tune out right there. I'm not saying it's not real, I'm saying that phrase makes people pick a team instead of listening.

DENISE: Agreed. And "our community" is vague. What do you actually want me to do? It sounds like a slogan, not help.

AALIYAH: See, but for me and my friends, "climate change" is the honest word. Don't sugarcoat it. If you dance around it we think you're hiding something. So that one actually works better for younger people.

FACILITATOR: Interesting — so the same words land very differently depending on who's hearing them. Here's the second.

Message B (heat/health framing): "Extreme heat can be deadly — especially for older adults, children, and people with health conditions. Here's how to stay safe this summer."

GLORIA: That one I like. It names who's at risk — that's me, that's my mother. And it says "here's how," so I expect you're about to actually tell me something useful.

MARCUS: Yeah, "children" and "health conditions" — that's my daughter. That gets my attention more than "the community."

JAMES: No politics in it. Just the facts. I'd keep reading.

ROSA: It's missing the flooding and the air, though. For my family heat isn't the only thing. But for a heat message, it's good.

FACILITATOR: Noted — strong on heat, but some of you experience this as more than heat. Here's the third.

Message C (protect-your-family / action framing): "Keep the people you love safe this summer. Free cooling centers, help with your energy bill, and home cooling tips — find what you need at [phone number / website]."

DENISE: Now that I'd act on. "Help with your energy bill" — say that louder. That's the thing keeping me from running my AC. You lead with the help, not the scary part.

JAMES: The bill help, yes. If that's real, I'm calling. That changes things for me.

TUAN: It's friendly. "People you love." But it's all about home — nothing for those of us working outside. I'd want a version that talks to workers.

AALIYAH: It's fine, it's a little — soft? For me. But I can see it working for families. And at least it tells you where to go, which the first one didn't.

GLORIA: The phone number matters. A lot of seniors aren't going to a website. Give them a person to call, and make sure somebody actually answers.

FACILITATOR: Let me try one more, focused on the longer-term side.

Message D (mitigation / collective framing): "More trees, cooler blocks, cleaner air. Join your neighbors to make our streets healthier for everyone."

AALIYAH: Yes. That's the one I'd show up for. It's hopeful, it's something to do together, not just protect yourself and hide inside.

ROSA: I like "cooler blocks." That's concrete. I can picture it.

MARCUS: Cleaner air gets me, because of my daughter. But part of me wonders — is this real or is it just nice words? If you say "join your neighbors," there better actually be a meeting, a planting day, something. Don't promise community and then it's just a flyer.

DENISE: Right. I've seen "more trees" promised before and nothing happened. So that message only works if you can point to something that's actually going to happen on my block.

JAMES: It's pretty, but it won't help me this August. That's a five-years-from-now message. I need both — the help now and the trees later.

FACILITATOR: That's a really important distinction — immediate help versus long-term change, and not letting one crowd out the other. If you had to tell us how to combine these, what would you say?

GLORIA: Lead with the help that's real today — the bill, the cooling, a number to call. Then tell people how to get involved in the bigger stuff. But don't make promises about trees you can't keep, because then we stop believing any of it.

TUAN: And make different versions. The family one for families, a workers' one for people like me, the climate one for the young folks. One message isn't going to fit everybody in this room — you can see that just from tonight.

AALIYAH: And put the young people to work on the long-term stuff. Tie the hope to an actual task.

ROSA: And whatever you say, say it in Spanish too, and the other languages on these blocks. My mother won't read an English flyer.

Part 4 — Trust and messengers

FACILITATOR: Last area. Who do you trust to bring you this information? Where would you actually believe it?

DENISE: My church. The pastor says it, people listen. A flyer from the city goes in the trash.

JAMES: My doctor. If my doctor tells me the heat is dangerous for my heart, that's different than the news telling me.

MARCUS: The school, for parents. If it comes home in my daughter's backpack, I read it.

GLORIA: The block captain, the people already checking on seniors. Use the networks that exist instead of building new ones.

ROSA: Someone who speaks my language and looks like my neighbors. Not a stranger in a city polo shirt who leaves after an hour.

AALIYAH: Honestly? Other young people, on the platforms we actually use. Not the city's official account that posts once a month.

TUAN: And someone who follows up. Everybody comes around once, asks questions like this, and you never hear back. Tell us what you did with what we said tonight. That's how you earn trust.

FACILITATOR: That's fair, and I'll take that one personally — we will share back what we heard. Any last thoughts before we close?

DENISE: Just — don't forget us once the summer's over and it's not in the news. The heat comes back every year now. So should the help.

GLORIA: And make it easy. People are tired. If it takes three phone calls and a website to get help with my bill, I'm not doing it. Make it one call.

AALIYAH: Give us something to do. Hope is a verb.

FACILITATOR: That's a perfect note to end on. Thank you all — this was incredibly valuable, and we're grateful for your time and your honesty.
